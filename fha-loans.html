FHA Loans

FHA loans are insured by the Federal Housing Administration and are often used by buyers who need a lower down payment option or more flexible credit guidelines.

How This Loan Works

FHA does not lend money directly to most borrowers. Instead, FHA-approved lenders make the loan, and FHA provides mortgage insurance that helps protect the lender if the borrower defaults. That insurance can make lenders more comfortable offering financing to borrowers who may not fit conventional loan guidelines.

FHA loans can allow down payments as low as 3.5 percent for eligible borrowers. They also tend to allow lower credit scores than many conventional loans, though borrowers must still qualify based on income, debt, property, loan limits, and lender requirements.

Why Borrowers Choose It

FHA financing can be helpful for first-time buyers, borrowers with smaller savings, or borrowers whose credit profile makes conventional financing more difficult or more expensive.

Important Tradeoffs

Mortgage insurance is required on FHA loans, and that cost is passed to the borrower. Depending on credit, down payment, and market conditions, an FHA loan may or may not be less expensive than a conventional loan.

Who It May Fit

FHA may fit borrowers who need flexible qualification standards, have limited funds for a down payment, or want to compare government-insured financing with conventional options. It is important to review both the monthly payment and total loan costs before choosing the program.
